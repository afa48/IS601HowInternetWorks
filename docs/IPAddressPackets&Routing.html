IP Address & Packets and Routing

  • IP Address Stands for Internet Protocol Address. Internet Protocol is a set of rules that govern the format of information sent via the internet. One example of Internet Protocol is The IP address. This is a unique address that identifies each device on the network. Each device has a set of numbers assigned to it. An example is such that when you send an email, one computer will send a message to another computer's IP address along with the origin address so that the receiving computer will know where to respond to.

  • Packets & Routing is the way that messages travel through the internet. One message is broken down into small packets of binary information. Packets travel using different routes and routes help keep the packets moving. When packets arrive, they may arrive at different times, and out of order. Packets are reassembled after they arrive. A protocol called TCP, Transmission Control Protocol does an inventory to make sure all the packets are received. TCP sends acknowledgements of every packet received. Packets keep getting sent until all are received. Once all are received, TCP signs off on it. The packets are reassembled at the destination. An example of this is when an email is sent, it is sent in small packets, or when we request a song on Spotify, it is sent in small packets and reassembled at the destination. When we hear the song, we have received it. Information on the internet can be sent as electronic pulses, light beams or radio waves. The information is sent the fastest through light beams using fiber optic cables that have been inserted into the grounds. The light beams bounce up and down the cable until they are received at the other end.
